Basics of Electricity


Definitions


Voltage (V) - Voltage is the electrical potential that pushes electrons through a circuit. Voltage is measured in volts (V).

Current (I) - Current can be described as the flow rate of electrons through a conductor. Current is measured in amperes (A)

Resistance(R) - Resistance is measured in ohms (Ω) and resists the flow of electricity.

Power (P) - Power is the rate at which electrical energy is used or produced. Power is measured in watts (W)

Direct Current (DC) - DC flows only in one direction with a constant polarity and magnitude. DC is procuded by batteries, solar panels, rectifiers and DC power supplies

Alternating Current (AC) - AC is electric current that changes direction (60 times per second in the US). The voltage will alternate in polarity, typically following a sine wave pattern. AC is easier to transmit over long distances with less power loss. Voltage can be stepped up or down using transformers.

Phase - Phase refers to the number of alternating current (AC) waveforms (or power lines) that deliver power. Each “phase” is a separate sine wave of voltage that alternates from positive to negative 60 times per second (60 Hz) in the U.S.

  • Single-phase - One AC sine wave
  • Three-phase - Three sin waves, each 120 degrees apart in time

Hertz (Hz) - Cycles per second. AC (Alternating Current) power supplies are 60 Hz in the United States

Ground - (also called earth) is a conductive connection between an electrical circuit and the earth’s surface, or a point that acts as zero volts within the system. It provides safety, equipment protection and voltage stabilization.

Load - The electrical load is any equipment using electricity

Short Circuit - A short circuit is an unintended low resistance path causing a high current.

Open Circuit - Electricity requires a closed circuit for the flow of electrons. An open circuit is also known as a broken circuit.

Circuit Breaker - A circuit breaker is a device that interrupts the flow of electricity during and overload


Ohm's Law


The formula can be rearranged to calculate any variable:

  • V = I x R (Voltage = Current x Resistance)
  • I = V/R
  • R = V/I

Power Law


P = V x I (Watts = Volts x Current)


Electrical Safety


  • Always lockout/tagout before working on electrical equipment
  • GFCIs should be used near water
  • Electrical panels and devices should be grounded
  • Never bypass breakers or fuses
  • Stay alert for arc flash and short circuit hazards
