Alkalinity & Hardness


Relationship of Alkalinity & Hardness

Alkalinity and hardness tests can be conducted to determine how much of the existing hardness is temporary and how much is permanent. These tests are necessary to determine checmical dosages in the treatment process


Alkalinity

Alkalinity measures water’s ability to neutralize acids and resist pH changes. It primarily results from the presence of bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻), carbonate (CO₃²⁻), and hydroxide (OH⁻) ions. Total alkalinity is determined by titrating a water sample with 0.02N sulfuric acid using two indicators: phenolphthalein and either methyl orange or bromcresol green. The phenolphthalein indicator measures P alkalinity — the portion due to hydroxide and half of the carbonate alkalinity—while the second indicator measures the total alkalinity. The difference between the two represents the bicarbonate portion. Alkalinity testing helps evaluate buffering capacity and ensures proper chemical balance in water treatment.


Hardness

Total hardness measures the concentration of dissolved calcium and magnesium ions in water, expressed as milligrams per liter (mg/L) as calcium carbonate. Hardness is determined by titration with an EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid) solution, which binds to calcium and magnesium ions. A CalVerindicator is added to the sample, producing a red color in the presence of hardness. As EDTA is titrated in, it reacts with the hardness ions until all are complexed, and the color changes from red to blue—indicating the endpoint. To measure calcium hardness specifically, a ManVer indicator is used along with a pH buffer that suppresses magnesium interference. The difference between total hardness and calcium hardness gives the magnesium hardness. Hardness testing is essential for controlling scaling, softening processes, and corrosion potential in water systems.
