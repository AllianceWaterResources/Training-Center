MO DNR Study Guide: Chlorine


Types of Chlorine

Calcium Hypochlorite

white granular tablet or powder (70% available chlorine, HTH)

Sodium Hypochlorite

yellowish liquid (bleach / generally 5% available chlorine)

Gaseous Chlorine

greenish yellow

Liquid Chlorine

amber color

Chlorine Disinfection Quick Facts

Essential Facts
  • Gaseous chlorine is 2.5 times heavier than air
  • 1 volume of liquid chlorine = 450 volumes of gas
  • Chlorine gas may ignite near petroleum products
  • Chlorine disinfects drinking water pathogens
  • Lower temperature slows chlorine disinfection
  • Lower pH increases chlorine effectiveness
  • Minimum contact time (CT) = 30 minutes
DPD Color Reaction

DPD reagent turns red in the presence of chlorine.

Chlorine Dosage

Dosage Definitions
  • Dosage: amount of chlorine added
  • Residual: amount of chlorine remaining after contact time
  • Demand: chlorine used up before any residual is attained
Key Formulas:
  • Chlorine Demand = Dosage − Residual
  • Dosage = Demand + Residual
  • Residual = Dosage − Demand

Breakpoint Chlorination: The point when chlorine demand is met and free chlorine remains as residual.

Chlorine Regulations

Minimum Chlorine Residuals
  • 0.5 ppm of free chlorine at the entrance to a distribution system
  • 1.0 ppm of chloramine at the entrance to a distribution system
  • These levels cannot fall below the minimum for more than 4 hours or the system must report to DNR by the next business day.
  • 0.2 ppm at any point in the distribution system
    (Cannot fall below this level for more than 4 hours — this is a Tier 2 violation)
Maximum Residual Disinfectant Levels (MRDL)
  • Chlorine – 4.0 ppm (based on RAA during bac-t sampling)
  • Chloramine – 4.0 ppm (based on RAA during bac-t sampling)

Chlorine Maximum Feed Rates

Feed Rate Limits
  • 150 lb. cylinder: 40 lbs/day
  • 1-ton container: 400 lbs/day
  • Rail car: 400 lbs/day

Handling Chlorine Containers

General Handling Guidelines
  • Turn valve stem off first to let vacuum draw out remaining chlorine
  • Use ammonia water to detect leaks — white cloud will form
  • Keep the wrench on the valve nut during operation
  • Transport/store 150 lb. cylinders upright
  • Use a scale to determine chlorine usage
  • Replace lead gasket every time a cylinder is connected
  • Open the valve counterclockwise one full turn
  • 1-ton containers can feed liquid (lower stem) or gas (upper stem)
  • Heat on a chlorine container is a sign of moisture contamination. Store below 140°F.
  • Use a special cradle — never use chains, ropes, or slings to lift.
Cylinder Securing

Full and empty 150 lb. cylinders must be secured with a chain 1/3 of the way down.

Fusible Plugs

Fusible plugs melt between 158–165°F to safely release pressure and prevent explosion.

Chlorine Storage Room Requirements

Room Design & Safety Requirements
  • SCBA: Must be stored outside the chlorine room in an unlocked cabinet with at least a 30-minute air supply.
  • Canister-type gas masks are not approved for chlorine leaks — even at low concentrations.
  • Emergency Exit: Room must have a panic bar on the door.
  • Ventilation: Fan must pull air from near the floor and be capable of changing the room’s air once per minute.

Chlorine Repair Kits

Match the Container with the Kit
  • 150 lb. cylinder: Repair Kit A
  • 1-ton container: Repair Kit B
  • Rail car: Repair Kit C

Chlorine Feed Equipment Terminology

Chlorinator

Regulates the flow of chlorine from the cylinder. Typically connects directly to the valve.

Vacuum Regulator

Controls the amount of chlorine gas pulled through the system. Often includes the rotameter.

Rotameter

A floating ball or cone inside a tube that indicates the chlorine gas usage rate in lbs/day.

Injector (Ejector)

Uses water flow to create a vacuum, opening the safety valve and drawing chlorine through the system.

A & B Exam – MCLs and Trivia

Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCL)
  • Trihalomethanes (TTHM): 0.08 ppm (RAA of all sites)
  • Haloacetic Acids (HAA5): 0.06 ppm (RAA)
  • Chlorite: 1.0 ppm (chlorine dioxide byproduct)
  • Bromate: 0.01 ppm (ozone byproduct)
  • Chlorine Dioxide MRDL: 0.8 ppm
    (Daily at entry point — no residual allowed in system)
  • HPC (Heterotrophic Plate Count): ≤ 500 colonies/mL
    (May substitute for daily chlorine testing)
Exam Trivia & Key Concepts
  • Humic and fulvic acids react with chlorine to form TTHM and HAA5
  • Chlorine removes taste and odor unless phenols are present
  • HOCl is the disinfecting form of chlorine
  • Evaporators and pressure reducers are required to convert liquid chlorine to gas
