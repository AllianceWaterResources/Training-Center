MO DNR Study Guide: Chlorine


Quick Facts

Calcium Hypochlorite is a white granular tablet or powder (70% available chlorine (HTH))

Sodium Hypochlorite is a yellowish liquid (bleach / generally 5% available chlorine)

Gaseous chlorine is greenish yellow

Liquid chlorine is an amber color

Gaseous chlorine is 2.5 times heavier than air

One liquid volume of chlorine is equal to 450 volumes of gaseous chlorine

Chlorine gas will ignite in the presence petroleum products

Chlorine is primarily used in water treatment to disinfect water of pathogens

Using the colorimeter method (DPD), the reagent will turn red in the presence of chlorine

A lower temperature slows down disinfection with chlorine

A lower pH will increase chlorine effectiveness to disinfect

The minimum contact time (CT) for chlorine is 30 minutes for adequate disinfection


Dosage

Dosage: amount of chlorine added

Residual: amount of chlorine remaining in the water after a certain contact time

Demand: amount of chlorine used up before any residual is attained

     Chlorine Demand = Dosage – Residual
     Dosage = Demand + Residual
     Residual = Dosage – Demand

Breakpoint Chlorination: the point when chlorine demand has been met and free chlorine remains as residual



Regulations

Lowest chlorine residuals allowable:

     0.5 ppm of free chlorine residual at the entrance to a distribution system

     1.0 ppm of chloramine residual at the entrance to a distribution system

     (Cannot fall below these levels for more than 4 hours or system must report to DNR by the end of the next business day)

     0.2 ppm at any point in the distribution system (Cannot fall below this level for more than 4 hours or this is a Tier 2 violation)

Maximum Residual Disinfectant Levels (MRDL) allowed in the system:

     Chlorine - 4.0 ppm (based upon RAA (Running Annual Average), as tested when bac-t sampling)

     Chloramine - 4.0 ppm (based upon RAA, as tested when bac-t sampling)


Chlorine Maximum Feed Rates


     150 lb. cylinder = 40 lbs/day
     1-ton container = 400 lbs/day
     Rail Car = 400 lbs/day



Handling

When shutting off chlorine feed: Turn cylinder valve stem off first and allow water vacuum to draw out remaining chlorine in the line

Locate chlorine leaks with ammonia water. A white cloud will appear in the presence of chlorine

When Chlorine feed is running always keep the wrench on the cylinder valve nut.

Chlorine cylinders (150 lbs.) should be transported and stored in an upright position

Empty and full cylinders (150 lbs.) must be secured with chains at 1/3 of the way down.

1-ton Containers require a special cradle to be lifted (never chains ropes or slings)

Heat from a chlorine cylinder or container is a sign of moisture contamination

Store below 140 degrees Fahrenheit

Fusible plugs melt between 158 and 165 degrees Fahrenheit. (fusible plugs allow a controlled release of chlorine gas rather than pressure building until it explodes)

The best way to determine chlorine usage is by using a scale

A new lead gasket should be placed on a cylinder / container every time you hook up a new unit to the regulator or yoke assembly.

Open the cylinder / container by rotating the valve stem in a counterclockwise direction one full turn

One ton containers can feed liquid or gas. (lower and upper stem)



Chlorine Storage Room Requirements

Self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA) kept outside chlorine room in unlocked cabinet with at least a 30 minute air supply (canister type gas masks are NOT designed for chlorine gas leaks regardless of how low the concentration might be.)

A panic bar on the door

Ventilation fan takes suction near the floor is capable of changing the rooms air once per minute



Repair Kits

150 lb. cylinder – Repair Kit A
1-ton container – Repair Kit B
Rail Car – Repair Kit C



Chlorine Feed Equipment Terminology

Chlorinator – regulate flow of chlorine from the cylinder. It’s usually hooked directly to valve of cylinder

Vacuum Regulator – controls the amount of chlorine gas pulled through the injector and serves as the housing for the rotameter

Rotameter – a ball or cone shaped devices that floats inside a glass tube. Indicates usage rate of chlorine gas in lbs/24 hours

Injector (also called ejector) – uses a water feed line to create a vacuum in the line , opens the safety valve on the chlorinator, and pulls chlorine gas through the vacuum regulator and out to the desired injection point



A & B Exam MCLs and Trivia

Trihalomethanes / TTHM = .08 ppm (based on running annual average of the results for all sampling sites)

Haloacidic Acids / HAA5 = .06 ppm (also based on RAA)

Chlorite = 1.0 ppm (This is a chlorine dioxide byproduct)

Bromate = .01 ppm (this is an Ozone byproduct)

Chlorine Dioxide MRDL – 0.8 ppm (Daily at entrance to distribution system – Cannot carry a residual in the distribution system)

HPC (Heterotrophic Plate Count) = less than or equal to 500 colonies per ml (might be used instead of daily chlorine testing)

Humic and fulvic acids combine with halogens (chlorine) in water and form TTHM and HAA5

Chlorine removes taste and Odor problems except in the presence of phenol compounds

HOCL (hypoclorous acid) is the chemical symbol that represents the disinfecting power CL2

To convert liquid chlorine into gas for chlorination, an evaporator and pressure reducer are required
